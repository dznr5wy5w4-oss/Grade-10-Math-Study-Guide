What you're walking into

The Nova Scotia Examination: Mathematics 10 (NSE Math 10) is worth 20% of your final course mark. It's written in June. The remaining 80% comes from your school work over the year.

The exam covers eight units built around HRCE's 2025–26 pacing. You'll be given a formula booklet on test day. The questions are mostly selected response (multiple choice + numerical) with some constructed response.

The four hot zones

Nova Scotia's Lessons Learned report flags four areas where the most students lose marks. Spend extra time on these:

Hot zone 1 · Negative exponents (AN03)

Historical success rate as low as 8%. Memorize: x⁻ⁿ = 1/xⁿ. Negative exponents move the base to the other side of the fraction bar — they don't make the answer negative.

Hot zone 2 · Factoring with concrete/pictorial models (AN05)

Students who memorize procedures without understanding algebra tiles miss these questions. Always pull out the greatest common factor first.

Hot zone 3 · How dimension changes affect SA & volume (M03)

If a length is multiplied by k, then SA × k², V × k³. Less than half of students get this right.

Hot zone 4 · Correlation coefficients (RF07)

r near +1 = strong positive, r near −1 = strong negative, r near 0 = no linear correlation. Strength uses absolute value.

Hot zone — correlation coefficient r

r is between −1 and +1. Sign = direction (positive/negative). |r| = strength: 0.9–1.0 strong, 0.7–0.9 moderate-strong, 0.5–0.7 moderate, <0.5 weak. r = 0 means no linear pattern.

Before applying any factoring pattern, look for a greatest common factor. Example: 4x² − 36 → 4(x² − 9) → 4(x + 3)(x − 3).

Hot zone — negative exponents (only ~8% historical success)

x⁻ⁿ ≠ −xⁿ. x⁻ⁿ means the reciprocal: x⁻ⁿ = 1/xⁿ. So 2⁻³ = 1/8, NOT −8.
Also: (−2)³ = −8 (negative answer); (−2)² = 4 (positive answer).

Test tips

The night before

  • Check your calculator. Set it to DEGREE mode for trig. Replace the battery if it's old. Bring a spare.
  • Sleep. Eight hours beats two extra hours of cramming on questions you mostly know.
  • Pack your bag: pencils, eraser, calculator, ruler, water bottle. Photo ID if your school requires it.
  • Review the Formulas tab one more time. Don't try to learn anything new the night before.

During the exam

  • Read every question twice. The biggest source of preventable mistakes is misreading the question.
  • Skim the whole exam first. Do the questions you're confident about first, then come back to harder ones. This builds momentum and protects your marks if time gets tight.
  • Show your work on constructed-response questions, even if the answer is wrong. Partial credit is real.
  • Check units: m vs cm, hours vs minutes, dollars vs cents. A correct number with wrong units gets marked wrong.
  • Sketch a diagram for trig and word problems. The act of drawing often shows you the answer.
  • If you get stuck, move on and come back. Don't burn 15 minutes on one question.

Hot zone strategy

The four hot zones from Lessons Learned are:

  1. Negative exponents: x⁻ⁿ = 1/xⁿ. Always rewrite as a fraction first. (−2)³ = −8 but (−2)² = +4.
  2. Factoring: pull GCF first, then identify the pattern (difference of squares, trinomial).
  3. SA & volume scaling: × k → SA × k², V × k³. Cube the scale factor for volume, square for surface area.
  4. Correlation r: sign tells direction, |r| tells strength. r = 0 means no linear relationship.

If you see a question on any of these, slow down. They're worth more than they look.

Common preventable mistakes

  • Calculator in radians instead of degrees
  • Squaring instead of square-rooting at the end of a Pythagorean problem
  • Forgetting the GCF in a factoring problem
  • Dividing percent change by the new value instead of the original
  • Reading a graph's scale wrong (each gridline = 2, not 1)
  • Solving for x but forgetting the question asked for y
  • Mixing imperial and SI units in the same calculation
  • Applying scale factor k to SA or V instead of k² and k³

Mindset

Anxiety eats time. Two slow breaths between questions if your heart is racing. The exam is a sample of what you've been doing all year — you've been preparing for it since September. Trust your work.

If you finish early, don't leave. Re-check your highest-mark questions first, then anything you flagged as uncertain.
