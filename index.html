What you're walking into

The Nova Scotia Examination: Mathematics 10 (NSE Math 10) is typically worth 20% of your final course mark (verify the current weighting with your teacher). It's written in June. The remaining mark comes from your school work over the year.

The exam covers eight units built around HRCE's 2025–26 pacing. You'll be given a formula booklet on test day. The questions are mostly selected response (multiple choice + numerical) with some constructed response.

The four hot zones

Nova Scotia's Lessons Learned report flags four areas where the most students lose marks. Spend extra time on these:

Hot zone 1 · Negative exponents (AN03)

Historical success rate as low as 8%. Memorize: x⁻ⁿ = 1/xⁿ. Negative exponents move the base to the other side of the fraction bar — they don't make the answer negative.

Hot zone 2 · Factoring with concrete/pictorial models (AN05)

Students who memorize procedures without understanding algebra tiles miss these questions. Always pull out the greatest common factor first.

Hot zone 3 · How dimension changes affect SA & volume (M03)

If a length is multiplied by k, then SA × k², V × k³. Less than half of students get this right.

Hot zone 4 · Correlation coefficients (RF07)

r near +1 = strong positive, r near −1 = strong negative, r near 0 = no linear correlation. Strength uses absolute value.

Hot zones — where the most marks are lost

The Nova Scotia Lessons Learned report flags four specific areas where students consistently underperform on the Math 10 exam. Spending extra time here pays back more than anywhere else.

Hot zone 1 · Negative exponents ~8% historical success

The rule: x⁻ⁿ = 1/xⁿ. A negative exponent does NOT make the answer negative — it takes the reciprocal.

ExpressionValueWhy
2⁻³1/81/2³ = 1/8 (positive!)
5⁻²1/251/5² = 1/25 (positive!)
(1/2)⁻³8Flip the fraction first: 2³ = 8
(−2)³−8Odd power of negative base = negative
(−2)²4Even power of negative base = positive
−2²−4Exponent only on 2; negative is OUTSIDE
(−2)⁻³−1/81/(−2)³ = 1/(−8) = −1/8
Strategy: when you see a negative exponent, rewrite the whole thing as a fraction first. Then evaluate.
Parentheses matter: (−2)² = 4 but −2² = −4. They're different problems.

Hot zone 2 · Factoring with concrete & pictorial models low success

The rule: always pull GCF first, then identify the pattern. Algebra tiles make this visual.

The factoring decision tree

  1. GCF first. Look at all terms, pull out the largest common factor (number AND variable parts).
  2. Two terms? Check for difference of squares: a² − b² = (a + b)(a − b). (Sum of squares does NOT factor.)
  3. Three terms with a = 1? Find two numbers that multiply to c and add to b.
  4. Three terms with a ≠ 1? Decomposition: ac, find ×=ac +=b, split, group.
  5. Always check: can any factor be factored further?
Worked example — full process
Factor 4x² − 36 completely.
Step 1 (GCF): 4 is common. → 4(x² − 9)
Step 2 (pattern): x² − 9 = x² − 3² is a difference of squares. → 4(x + 3)(x − 3)
Step 3 (check): each factor is linear, can't go further. ✓

Algebra tiles — visual factoring

Three tile sizes:

  • Large square = x²
  • Long rectangle = x
  • Small square = 1

To factor x² + 5x + 6 with tiles: take 1 large square + 5 long rectangles + 6 small squares. Try to arrange them into a single rectangle.

       x        1   1   1
   ┌───────┬───┬───┬───┐
   │       │   │   │   │
 x │  x²   │ x │ x │ x │
   │       │   │   │   │
   ├───────┼───┼───┼───┤
 1 │   x   │ 1 │ 1 │ 1 │
   ├───────┼───┼───┼───┤
 1 │   x   │ 1 │ 1 │ 1 │
   └───────┴───┴───┴───┘
       Width = (x + 3)
       Length = (x + 2)

The dimensions of the rectangle are the factors: (x + 2)(x + 3). If you can't form a rectangle, the polynomial doesn't factor (with integer coefficients).

Skipping the GCF is the most common factoring error. Always look for it before trying any pattern.

Hot zone 3 · How dimension changes affect SA & volume ~50% success

The rule: if every length is multiplied by k:

  • Surface area is multiplied by k²
  • Volume is multiplied by k³

Why? Surface area is two-dimensional (length × length), so two factors of k. Volume is three-dimensional (length × length × length), so three factors of k.

Scale factor kSA changes byV changes by
2 (double)× 4× 8
3 (triple)× 9× 27
4× 16× 64
1/2 (half)× 1/4× 1/8
1/3× 1/9× 1/27
Worked example
A sphere has radius 4 cm and volume V₀. If the radius is doubled to 8 cm, the new volume is:
V_new = V₀ × 2³ = 8 V₀
The new SA is the original SA × 2² = 4 × original SA.
The most common wrong answer multiplies SA or V by k itself. SA scales as k², V as k³ — don't skip the squaring or cubing.

Hot zone 4 · Correlation coefficient r

The rule: r tells you about the LINEAR relationship between two variables in a scatter plot. Range: −1 ≤ r ≤ 1.

Aspect of rWhat it tells you
Sign (+/−)Direction: + means both variables increase together; − means as one increases, the other decreases
|r| (absolute value)Strength: the closer to 1, the stronger the linear pattern
r = 0NO linear correlation (could still have a curved pattern)

Strength scale

|r|Strength
0.9 – 1.0Strong
0.7 – 0.9Moderate-strong
0.5 – 0.7Moderate
< 0.5Weak
Reading r values
• r = 0.94 → strong positive (close to perfect upward line)
• r = −0.82 → moderate-strong negative (clear downward pattern, some scatter)
• r = 0.30 → weak positive (slight upward tendency)
• r = 0 → no linear pattern (data could still be related — just not by a line)
Correlation does NOT mean causation. Two strongly correlated variables don't prove one causes the other.

Test tips

The night before

  • Check your calculator. Set it to DEGREE mode for trig. Replace the battery if it's old. Bring a spare.
  • Sleep. Eight hours beats two extra hours of cramming on questions you mostly know.
  • Pack your bag: pencils, eraser, calculator, ruler, water bottle. Photo ID if your school requires it.
  • Review the Formulas tab one more time. Don't try to learn anything new the night before.

During the exam

  • Read every question twice. The biggest source of preventable mistakes is misreading the question.
  • Skim the whole exam first. Do the questions you're confident about first, then come back to harder ones. This builds momentum and protects your marks if time gets tight.
  • Show your work on constructed-response questions, even if the answer is wrong. Partial credit is real.
  • Check units: m vs cm, hours vs minutes, dollars vs cents. A correct number with wrong units gets marked wrong.
  • Sketch a diagram for trig and word problems. The act of drawing often shows you the answer.
  • If you get stuck, move on and come back. Don't burn 15 minutes on one question.

Hot zone strategy

The four hot zones from Lessons Learned are:

  1. Negative exponents: x⁻ⁿ = 1/xⁿ. Always rewrite as a fraction first. (−2)³ = −8 but (−2)² = +4.
  2. Factoring: pull GCF first, then identify the pattern (difference of squares, trinomial).
  3. SA & volume scaling: × k → SA × k², V × k³. Cube the scale factor for volume, square for surface area.
  4. Correlation r: sign tells direction, |r| tells strength. r = 0 means no linear relationship.

If you see a question on any of these, slow down. They're worth more than they look.

Common preventable mistakes

  • Calculator in radians instead of degrees
  • Squaring instead of square-rooting at the end of a Pythagorean problem
  • Forgetting the GCF in a factoring problem
  • Dividing percent change by the new value instead of the original
  • Reading a graph's scale wrong (each gridline = 2, not 1)
  • Solving for x but forgetting the question asked for y
  • Mixing imperial and SI units in the same calculation
  • Applying scale factor k to SA or V instead of k² and k³

Mindset

Anxiety eats time. Two slow breaths between questions if your heart is racing. The exam is a sample of what you've been doing all year — you've been preparing for it since September. Trust your work.

If you finish early, don't leave. Re-check your highest-mark questions first, then anything you flagged as uncertain.
